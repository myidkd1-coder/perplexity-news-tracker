IMF Projects Fiscal Tightrope for India's Budget

The International Monetary Fund (IMF) has cast a cautious outlook on India's central government finances, projecting that the forthcoming 2026-27 budget will navigate a challenging fiscal path. The primary headwinds identified are softer revenue generation and mounting interest costs on sovereign debt, demanding a delicate balancing act for policymakers.

Revenue Pressures Mount, Spending Realigned

Government income, measured as a percentage of GDP, is estimated to slightly decline from 9 percent in the current fiscal year to 8.9 percent in 2026-27. Tax collections are expected to hold steady at 7.2 percent of GDP, with non-tax revenues contributing 1.7 percent. The dip in overall revenue is partly attributed to lower tax rates aimed at stimulating consumer spending. To compensate, efforts may intensify to broaden the tax base.

Total government spending is slated for a reduction, falling from 14.1 percent of GDP in 2024-25 to 13.4 percent in 2026-27. Within this consolidated spending, interest payments on loans are set to increase marginally from 3.4 percent to 3.6 percent of GDP. Subsidies are projected to decrease from 1.2 percent to 1.1 percent, and infrastructure spending will see a slight cut, dropping from 3.2 percent to 3 percent of GDP. This recalibration suggests a potential need for innovative funding mechanisms for infrastructure, such as asset monetization or public-private partnerships.

Deficit Narrowing Central, Widening Combined

The fiscal deficit at the central government level is anticipated to improve, decreasing from 4.9 percent of GDP in 2024-25 to 4.5 percent in 2026-27, aligning with the budgeted 4.4 percent for the current fiscal year. The IMF expects no further significant reduction in the deficit in the medium term. Government debt is projected to decrease from 56.2 percent of GDP to 55.4 percent by 2026-27, a positive signal for financial health.

However, when considering both central and state government finances combined, the overall deficit is forecast to rise slightly from 7.1 percent to 7.2 percent of GDP in 2026-27. This could be a contributing factor to persistent high long-term bond yields. Across all government levels, interest payments are expected to climb from 4.8 percent to 5.5 percent of GDP, a development the IMF views with concern, calling for "ambitious medium-term fiscal consolidation" to rebuild buffers and support growth-enhancing expenditures.
